# Architecture Specification: The Moore Kernel, Brief Language, and FPGA Hardware Mounting

## 1. Architectural Context and Paradigm Shift

The demand for extreme-scale computing, driven by massive datasets, real-time cyber-physical systems, and cryptographic acceleration, has increasingly exposed the limitations of traditional, static processor architectures. As conventional scaling hits hard power and energy limits, the confluence of changes required in both hardware and software has created an imperative for advanced hardware-software co-design [cite: 1]. In this emerging paradigm, the strict demarcation between software execution and hardware logic is dissolving. Systems must dynamically adapt to workloads by physically reconfiguring their computational substrates at runtime, achieving the flexibility of software combined with the determinism and throughput of application-specific integrated circuits (ASICs) [cite: 2, 3].

This specification details a tripartite architectural framework designed to address these challenges. The architecture introduces a novel operating system model known as the Moore Kernel, which orchestrates reconfigurable computing resources by treating hardware processes as first-class citizens. To ensure the safety and functional correctness of the dynamic logic deployed by the kernel, the architecture employs the Brief language, a formal, contract-based hardware specification dialect. Finally, to facilitate high-bandwidth, deterministic communication between the reconfigurable logic and external physical interfaces, the specification mandates an interconnect strategy utilizing the Aurora 64B/66B protocol over standard Category 6 (Cat6) cabling via Low-Voltage Differential Signaling (LVDS). This document serves as the foundational blueprint, intended to be integrated into a version-controlled repository utilizing standardized Architecture Decision Records (ADRs) to govern future development.

## 2. Epistemological Foundations of the Moore Kernel

The conceptual underpinning of the Moore Kernel represents a radical departure from traditional operating system design. Conventional operating systems rely heavily on abstraction and virtualization, effectively hiding the physical realities of the hardware behind layers of software emulation. This approach parallels philosophical idealism, wherein reality is deemed to be a mental construct, and the "external world" is inaccessible. The Moore Kernel rejects this paradigm in favor of "digital realism," heavily influenced by the early twentieth-century philosopher G.E. Moore.

### 2.1. Digital Realism and Common Sense Philosophy

In his seminal works, "A Defence of Common Sense" and "Proof of an External World," G.E. Moore established a defense of reality against skeptical idealism by relying on "truisms" [cite: 4, 5]. Moore famously proved the existence of the external, mind-independent world by holding up his hands and declaring, "Here is one hand, and here is another," asserting that physical objects exist independently of human perception or abstract theorizing [cite: 6]. 

The Moore Kernel translates this epistemological framework directly into hardware management. Rather than treating hardware as an abstract, virtualized resource pool, the kernel enforces a direct realist interaction with the Field-Programmable Gate Array (FPGA) fabric. When the kernel allocates a hardware process, it asserts the physical existence of that process [cite: 7, 8]. The hardware process is not a virtual thread occupying a timeslice on a general-purpose CPU; it is a physical manifestation of logic gates, interconnected wires, and spatial geometry on the silicon die. The kernel validates the existence of these external hardware tasks through direct polling of memory-mapped interfaces and physical status registers, achieving an operational certainty analogous to Moore's proof of his own hands [cite: 6, 9]. 

Moore's philosophy also grappled with the concept of "sense-data"—the raw, uninterpreted information received by the senses, which forms the basis of our perceptual knowledge of the external world [cite: 6, 8]. In the context of the Moore Kernel, sense-data corresponds to the telemetry, interrupts, and register values generated by the physical hardware modules. While traditional systems might buffer, mask, or virtualize these signals, the Moore Kernel relies on direct abductive inference from this hardware "sense-data" to make deterministic scheduling and allocation decisions, ensuring that the software state remains perfectly synchronized with the true physical state of the machine [cite: 10].

### 2.2. The Hardware Process Abstraction

In reconfigurable hardware operating systems, the "hardware task" or "hardware process" is the primary abstraction used to represent application functionality in a device-independent manner [cite: 11]. The Moore Kernel extends the traditional UNIX process model to encompass these physical circuits, providing a homogeneous interface for both software and hardware execution [cite: 12, 13]. 

Hardware tasks possess unique characteristics that differentiate them from software threads. While the functional characteristic of the task (its specific algorithmic behavior) is opaque to the operating system, its structural and timing characteristics are fully exposed [cite: 14]. Structural characteristics include the physical area required on the fabric, measured in Reconfigurable Units (RCUs), and the spatial shape of the circuit [cite: 14]. Timing characteristics dictate the clock domain requirements and the execution latency. The Moore Kernel utilizes these physical parameters to perform advanced two-dimensional (2D) spatial scheduling, which has been shown to be vastly superior to one-dimensional (1D) resource models in optimizing the placement of dynamic tasks on dense FPGA arrays [cite: 14].

### 2.3. Task Lifecycle and State Machine Orchestration

The lifecycle of a hardware process within the Moore Kernel is governed by a rigorous state machine, ensuring that physical resources are managed securely and efficiently. During the design phase, applications are decomposed into position-independent task circuits and stored in a Raw Task Repository [cite: 11]. When execution is requested, the kernel initiates a complex sequence of physical operations.

The Task Preparation Unit (TPU) retrieves the raw task from the repository and performs a relocation procedure [cite: 11]. Relocation involves modifying the raw bitstream to target a specific spatial coordinate within the FPGA's user area, generating a localized partial bitstream ready for download [cite: 11]. Once prepared, the kernel dynamically allocates the required spatial footprint and streams the bitstream to the configuration port [cite: 11].

Communication between the executing hardware process and the kernel is standardized through a Standard Task Interface (STI) [cite: 11, 14]. The STI exposes essential control signals that the kernel manipulates to manage the execution state. The Reset (RST) signal is utilized to initialize the circuit safely post-configuration. The Enable (ENA) signal starts and stops the clocking or data flow to the task. Finally, the Finished (FIN) signal is asserted by the hardware task upon completion, acting as a hardware interrupt that triggers the kernel's scheduler to reclaim the physical resources [cite: 11, 14].

| Lifecycle Phase | Kernel Subsystem | Operation Description | Hardware Process State |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Repository Storage | File System | Raw, position-independent bitstream stored on disk. | Dormant |
| Preparation | Task Preparation Unit (TPU) | Calculates 2D spatial coordinates and patches bitstream for relocation. | Pending Allocation |
| Configuration | Configuration Packet Processor | Bitstream streamed via ICAP/PCAP to the physical fabric. | Configuring |
| Execution | Task Scheduler | Asserts RST for initialization, then ENA to begin processing. | Active |
| Preemption | Resource Manager | Halts clock, performs context extraction to save internal state. | Suspended |
| Termination | Interrupt Controller | Receives FIN signal, unmounts bitstream, reclaims RCUs. | Completed |

To support true multitasking, the Moore Kernel implements hardware preemption. When a higher-priority task requires physical resources currently occupied by an executing process, the kernel initiates a context extraction sequence [cite: 11, 15]. This involves halting the execution, reading back the configuration memory and flip-flop states from the active region, and saving this context to main memory [cite: 11]. The physical area is then reconfigured. When the preempted task is ready to resume, the TPU performs context insertion, merging the saved state data with the original raw task bitstream before remounting it, allowing the process to resume exactly where it was interrupted [cite: 11].

## 3. Dynamic Function eXchange and Physical Orchestration

The physical mechanism enabling the Moore Kernel to mount, preempt, and unmount hardware processes dynamically is Dynamic Function eXchange (DFX), a technology central to modern AMD Zynq UltraScale+ architectures [cite: 16, 17, 18]. DFX allows specific regions of an FPGA, designated as Reconfigurable Partitions (RP), to be reprogrammed with new Reconfigurable Modules (RM) while the surrounding static logic continues to operate without interruption [cite: 17, 19]. 

### 3.1. Virtual Sockets and Configuration Interfaces

The Moore Kernel manages the FPGA fabric by defining an array of virtual sockets, each corresponding to a Reconfigurable Partition. The architecture is inherently "slotted," allowing for dynamically composable hardware systems without the necessity of recompiling a monolithic configuration bitstream [cite: 18]. This decoupled lifecycle facilitates the deployment of new hardware accelerators to the field long after the base platform has been deployed [cite: 18].

To deliver partial bitstreams to these virtual sockets, the kernel utilizes dedicated configuration interfaces. The primary mechanism on Zynq MPSoC devices is the Processor Configuration Access Port (PCAP), which allows the software-based kernel executing on the processing system (PS) to drive bitstreams directly into the programmable logic (PL) via direct memory access (DMA) engines [cite: 20]. For architectures demanding strict isolation between the software control plane and the hardware datapath, the kernel can alternatively utilize the Internal Configuration Access Port (ICAP) [cite: 20, 21]. By driving the ICAP from within the static programmable logic itself, the system enables autonomous, hardware-managed reconfiguration that operates entirely independently of the CPU, achieving microsecond-level context switching [cite: 20].

### 3.2. Decoupling and Blanking Bitstreams

The physical alteration of silicon logic gates at runtime introduces significant electrical and logical hazards. During the process of loading a partial bitstream, the physical connections between the static logic and the reconfigurable partition enter an unpredictable state [cite: 22]. Consequently, the output ports of the partition undergoing reconfiguration may generate random patterns, illegal values, or floating electrical states that could catastrophically corrupt the execution of the active static logic [cite: 22].

To mitigate this, the Moore Kernel strictly enforces a decoupling protocol. Prior to any reconfiguration event, the kernel activates a Dynamic Function eXchange Decoupler IP block [cite: 17]. This decoupler erects a logical isolation barrier, clamping all signals crossing the partition boundary to predefined safe values (typically logic low or high) [cite: 22]. The decoupler ensures that the static logic remains entirely insulated from the electrical chaos occurring within the partition during bitstream delivery [cite: 22].

When a hardware process completes and the kernel needs to reclaim the virtual socket, the process must be formally "unmounted." Unmounting involves transitioning the partition to a neutral, power-efficient state using a "blanking bitstream," also referred to in Vivado toolchains as a greybox bitstream [cite: 23]. A blanking bitstream removes the functional logic of the active module and replaces it exclusively with tie-off look-up tables (LUTs) [cite: 23]. These tie-offs ensure that all module I/O ports are persistently driven to ground (or Vcc, if specifically configured via the `HD.PARTPIN_TIEOFF` property) [cite: 23]. 

The complete unmount operation executed by the Moore Kernel follows a precise, fault-tolerant sequence [cite: 22]:

1.  **Safe State Transition:** The kernel commands the executing hardware process to flush its pipelines and enter a safe, idle state to prevent data corruption.
2.  **Reset Assertion:** The kernel activates the reset signals specifically routed to the reconfigurable partition.
3.  **Decoupler Activation:** The DFX Decoupler is engaged, electrically isolating the partition from the static system.
4.  **Bitstream Delivery:** The compressed blanking bitstream is fetched from the repository and streamed into the ICAP or PCAP [cite: 22, 23].
5.  **STARTUP Sequence:** The kernel waits for the internal FPGA STARTUP sequence to finalize, ensuring that all synchronous elements within the newly blanked region are initialized to default values [cite: 22].
6.  **Decoupler Deactivation:** The logical barrier is removed. Because the blanking bitstream provides safe tie-off values, the static logic experiences no glitching.
7.  **Reset Deactivation:** The reset signals are lifted, leaving the partition in a clean, dormant state ready for future allocation.

## 4. The Brief Language: Formal Contracts for Hardware Specifications

While the Moore Kernel provides the operational framework for dynamic hardware execution, ensuring the functional correctness and security of the modules it orchestrates is equally critical. The immense complexity of modern Very Large Scale Integration (VLSI) designs renders traditional simulation-based verification insufficient. Simulating a digital system exhaustively for even a modest input size is computationally prohibitive; a simple 32-bit comparator requires $2^{64}$ unique test vectors, which would take hundreds of thousands of years to verify on standard simulation engines [cite: 24]. Consequently, modern hardware verification relies on formal methods, and this architecture mandates the use of the "Brief" language for all hardware design.

### 4.1. Contract-Based Design and Semantics

Brief is a high-level hardware construction language built around the paradigm of formal contracts. Unlike traditional Hardware Description Languages (HDLs) such as Verilog and VHDL, which focus on implementing cycle-accurate register transfers and structural wiring, Brief operates at a pre-algorithmic abstraction level [cite: 25, 26]. It separates the specification of *what* a system must achieve from the implementation details of *how* it operates [cite: 25, 27].

The core construct of Brief is the formal contract, a mathematically precise agreement between a hardware module and its environment (the Moore Kernel) [cite: 25, 28]. Contracts are defined using preconditions, which the environment must satisfy before invoking the hardware, and postconditions, which the hardware guarantees to satisfy upon completion [cite: 28, 29]. State transitions are formally captured by the equation $s' = \text{op}(s)$, where $s$ is the initial input state, $\text{op}$ is the hardware operation, and $s'$ is the resulting output state [cite: 25]. By embedding these predicative specifications directly into the language syntax, Brief ensures that designs are "correct by construction" [cite: 27]. 

The syntax of Brief incorporates Linear Temporal Logic (LTL) to reason about the evolution of hardware states over multiple clock cycles [cite: 30, 31]. Digital circuits are inherently reactive, time-dependent systems. Standard propositional logic cannot capture requirements such as "Request A must eventually be followed by Acknowledge B." Brief extends the standard Boolean operators (AND, OR, XOR, NOT) with temporal operators that evaluate properties over sequential execution paths [cite: 30]. This allows developers to define invariant properties—conditions that must hold true globally, without exception, such as ensuring a DMA memory buffer never overflows [cite: 25, 32]. A deterministic state machine written in Verilog to monitor such rules requires exponentially more logic resources than the concise LTL assertions natively compiled by Brief [cite: 30].

### 4.2. Automated Theorem Proving and Model Checking

The Brief compiler is deeply integrated with a formal verification toolchain that exhaustively proves the adherence of the hardware implementation to its contractual specification [cite: 33, 34]. The verification strategy dynamically adapts to the class of the circuit being synthesized.

For combinational circuits, which lack internal state or feedback loops, the verification process is entirely automated [cite: 35]. The Brief compiler translates the high-level specification and the resulting RTL into first-order logic formulas. It employs a deductive calculus to prove equivalence by unwinding internal wire definitions, pruning existentially quantified variables, and utilizing rewriting tactics to reduce the circuit to base Boolean operators [cite: 35]. If the Boolean expressions of the specification and implementation match, the circuit is mathematically proven correct.

Sequential circuits, which represent state transitions over time, require more sophisticated bounded model checking techniques [cite: 34, 35]. The compiler generates an abstract state-transition system from the Brief code [cite: 32, 34]. Boolean Satisfiability (SAT) solvers and Satisfiability Modulo Theories (SMT) solvers are then deployed to search for counterexamples that might violate the temporal LTL contracts [cite: 31, 34]. If the SAT solver identifies a valid state path that violates a safety property or fails to reach a liveness property, the compilation is aborted, and the developer is provided with a cycle-accurate trace demonstrating the failure [cite: 24, 31].

For highly complex, iterated circuits—such as cascaded cryptographic hash functions or deep neural network accelerators consisting of $n$ identical modules—the state-space explosion often defeats automated model checkers. In these scenarios, Brief leverages higher-order logic and mathematical induction [cite: 35]. Developers can guide the theorem prover by providing lemmas and arithmetic theorems, using inductive tactics (e.g., `INDUCT-TAC`) to prove equivalence across an arbitrary number of recursive instantiations [cite: 35].

| Verification Target | Circuit Characteristics | Mathematical Framework | Toolchain Mechanism |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Combinational Logic | Stateless, purely functional mapping of inputs to outputs. | First-Order Predicate Logic | Automated Boolean rewriting, unwinding, and equivalence checking. |
| Sequential Logic | State-dependent, reliant on clock cycles and memory elements. | Linear Temporal Logic (LTL) | Bounded Model Checking (BMC) via SAT/SMT solvers searching for counterexamples. |
| Iterated Logic | Cascaded, recursive structures of identical sub-modules. | Higher-Order Logic | Semi-automated inductive proofs guided by developer-provided lemmas. |

### 4.3. Microarchitectural Leakage Contracts for Security

As the Moore Kernel operates as a multi-tenant operating system, dynamically swapping hardware processes from mutually untrusting applications, side-channel security is paramount. A malicious hardware module could monitor shared resources—such as cache hit/miss latencies, power consumption fluctuations, or bus contention—to exfiltrate secret keys from an adjacent reconfigurable partition [cite: 36, 37].

To mitigate this, the Brief language introduces the concept of microarchitectural leakage contracts [cite: 37, 38]. These contracts extend traditional functional verification to guarantee that a hardware module does not leak sensitive information through its microarchitectural execution paths ($\mu$PATH) [cite: 37].

A leakage contract atom is formally defined as a tuple $A = (\pi_A, \tau_A, \phi_A)$ [cite: 38, 39]. In this formulation:
*   $\pi_A$ is the predicate that determines if the observation is applicable in the current architectural state.
*   $\tau_A$ is the identifier for the specific physical leakage source (e.g., the latency of a division unit, or the address bus of a memory interface).
*   $\phi_A$ is the observation function that maps the internal architectural state to the externally observable leakage trace [cite: 38, 39].

The Brief compiler synthesizes a complete contract template comprising all potential leakage atoms within the design [cite: 38, 39]. It then performs a symbolic information flow analysis across all execution test-cases [cite: 37, 38]. If the compiler determines that a piece of data designated as "secret" within the contract influences the observable trace $\phi_A$—meaning that an attacker could distinguish the secret value by measuring the execution latency or power state—the synthesis fails [cite: 37, 38]. This rigorous enforcement ensures that any bitstream mounted by the Moore Kernel is mathematically guaranteed to be side-channel secure, preserving the isolation integrity of the multi-tenant physical fabric [cite: 39].

## 5. Physical Infrastructure: LVDS and Aurora 64B/66B over Cat6

The epistemological realism of the Moore Kernel and the formal proofs of the Brief language converge in their application upon the physical hardware interconnects. Modern cyber-physical systems, such as automotive radar processing, high-frequency trading platforms, and hardware-in-the-loop (HIL) simulators, require massive data ingestion bandwidth combined with deterministic, ultra-low latency [cite: 2, 40]. Traditional networking stacks, such as Gigabit Ethernet relying on TCP/IP processing, introduce unacceptable software overhead and unpredictable latency spikes that defeat the purpose of hardware acceleration [cite: 41, 42]. 

Consequently, this architecture mandates a specialized physical interconnect strategy: utilizing the Aurora 64B/66B protocol transmitted via Low-Voltage Differential Signaling (LVDS) over standard Category 6 (Cat6) twisted-pair RJ45 cabling [cite: 43, 44].

### 5.1. The Aurora 64B/66B Protocol Architecture

Aurora 64B/66B is a scalable, lightweight, link-layer protocol designed by Xilinx (now AMD) specifically for point-to-point, high-speed serial communication between programmable logic devices [cite: 44, 45]. By operating directly adjacent to the physical layer transceivers and exposing a standard AXI4-Stream interface to the user logic, Aurora bypasses complex addressing and routing layers, allowing the Moore Kernel to stream sensor data directly into the virtual sockets [cite: 43, 44].

The protocol achieves exceptional throughput efficiency by utilizing the 64B/66B encoding scheme defined by the IEEE 802.3ae standard [cite: 46, 47]. In this scheme, 64 bits of payload data are appended with a 2-bit synchronization header [cite: 46]. This header distinguishes between data blocks and control blocks, while the payload undergoes a self-scrambling algorithm to ensure sufficient clock recovery transitions regardless of the user data pattern [cite: 46]. The 64B/66B encoding results in a transmission overhead of merely 3% [cite: 47]. This is a massive improvement over older, simpler protocols relying on 8B/10B encoding, which suffer from a debilitating 25% overhead penalty [cite: 45, 47].

Within the FPGA logic, the Aurora TX lane consists of an encoder, a 64/66 scrambler, a 64/66 gearbox, and a serializer [cite: 46]. The gearbox manages the alignment of the 66-bit blocks across the physical interface width [cite: 46]. On the receiving end, the RX lane performs deserialization, block synchronization, descrambling, and channel bonding [cite: 46]. Channel bonding is particularly critical when implementing multi-lane Aurora links; the core can bond multiple physical twisted pairs together to act as a single logical channel, automatically compensating for up to two symbols of skew between the physical lanes caused by varying cable lengths or PCB trace discrepancies [cite: 45, 47].

### 5.2. Implementing LVDS over Cat6 Infrastructure

Deploying multi-gigabit transceivers (such as the GTH or GTY blocks found on Zynq UltraScale+ devices) over standard Cat6 copper infrastructure introduces unique signal integrity and clocking challenges compared to optical fiber or dedicated twinax cables [cite: 46, 47]. Cat6 cabling consists of four unshielded twisted pairs (UTP), making it an ideal physical medium for Low-Voltage Differential Signaling (LVDS) [cite: 42]. LVDS transmits data as a voltage difference between the two wires of a twisted pair, providing inherent immunity to common-mode electrical noise and electromagnetic interference (EMI).

However, Cat6 is highly susceptible to high-frequency attenuation and signal jitter at extended lengths. Therefore, the architecture requires meticulous configuration of the physical layer clock domains and robust reset management. 

When establishing a 5 Gbps line rate over the copper link, the internal clocking architecture must be precisely synchronized. The design instantiates a Clocking Wizard primitive configured as a Mixed-Mode Clock Manager (MMCM) [cite: 40]. The MMCM derives its input from the transceiver's recovered `tx_out_clk`. For a 5 Gbps line rate feeding a 32-bit internal datapath, the `tx_out_clk` frequency is calculated as $5 \text{ Gbps} / 32 = 156.25 \text{ MHz}$ [cite: 40]. The MMCM utilizes this base frequency to generate two distinct clock domains: the `user_clk`, which drives the AXI4-Stream interfaces exposing the data to the Moore Kernel's hardware processes, and the `sync_clk`, which drives the internal synchronizer flip-flops of the Aurora core itself [cite: 40].

Because copper cables in industrial or laboratory environments are prone to physical disconnection or severe transient noise, the system cannot rely on software interrupts for link recovery. The architecture dictates the implementation of a dedicated hardware-based reset state machine [cite: 43]. This logic utilizes the AMD Processor System Reset IP block, combining the initialization clock (`init_clk`) and the physical media attachment initialization (`pma_init`) signals [cite: 40]. In the event of excessive bit errors, a complete loss of clock lock, or physical disconnection, the state machine automatically asserts a catastrophic reset, flushing all internal FIFOs and forcing the transceiver back into its equalization and training state [cite: 43, 45]. Once physical continuity is restored, the hardware automatically re-establishes channel bonding and resumes data streaming, ensuring that the Moore Kernel experiences minimal disruption [cite: 43, 45].

## 6. Repository Standards and Architecture Decision Records

To ensure that this highly complex, multi-disciplinary architecture is successfully integrated into a version-controlled repository, strict documentation standards must be enforced. Hardware-software co-design projects frequently suffer from architectural amnesia, where the rationale behind critical physical layer constraints or kernel scheduling decisions is lost over time, leading to disastrous refactoring attempts [cite: 48, 49]. To prevent this, the project repository will mandate the use of Requests for Comments (RFCs) for proposing system changes, and Architecture Decision Records (ADRs) for permanently logging adopted designs [cite: 50, 51].

### 6.1. The MADR Framework

The repository will standardize on the Markdown Any Decision Record (MADR) format. MADR provides a lightweight, easily parsable structure that is native to version control environments like GitHub and GitLab [cite: 52, 53]. Unlike verbose traditional documentation, MADR focuses intensely on the decision context, the options considered, the final choice, and the resulting trade-offs (consequences) [cite: 49, 53]. 

The structural format enforces the inclusion of critical metadata, including the decision status (e.g., Proposed, Accepted, Superseded) and the exact date of resolution [cite: 53]. ADR files are stored sequentially in a dedicated `/docs/decisions/` directory, adhering to a strict naming convention such as `NNNN-title-of-decision.md` (e.g., `0004-adopt-aurora-protocol.md`) [cite: 52, 54]. This creates an immutable, chronological ledger of the system's architectural evolution, providing invaluable context for future engineers onboarding to the project [cite: 48, 49].

## 7. Synthesis

The architecture outlined in this specification represents a comprehensive framework for next-generation, dynamically reconfigurable computing. By adopting the epistemological tenets of digital realism, the Moore Kernel establishes a robust operating system paradigm where physical hardware processes are managed with the same operational fluidity as software threads. The integration of Dynamic Function eXchange (DFX), virtual sockets, decoupling barriers, and blanking bitstreams ensures that the continuous reconfiguration of the FPGA fabric occurs without compromising the stability of the static system.

To guarantee that the volatile logic modules orchestrated by the kernel are functionally sound and secure, the architecture mandates the Brief formal specification language. By embedding Linear Temporal Logic contracts and side-channel leakage atoms directly into the development cycle, Brief ensures that all generated hardware is mathematically proven against catastrophic failure modes and security vulnerabilities before synthesis. Finally, the physical deployment strategy leverages the low-overhead Aurora 64B/66B protocol mapped over standard Cat6 LVDS copper cabling. This provides the deterministic, multi-gigabit ingestion bandwidth required by modern cyber-physical and high-performance computing applications, bypassing traditional network stack bottlenecks.

By strictly capturing these complex interconnect, software, and hardware design decisions within standard Markdown Architecture Decision Records (MADRs), the development team ensures that the rationale driving the Moore Kernel, the Brief language, and the physical interconnect strategy remains immutably documented, providing a stable foundation for all future iterations of the project.
